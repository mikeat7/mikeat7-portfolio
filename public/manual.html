1 · The Big Picture

Clarity Armor is a website with one core belief: language models sound confident whether or not they are right, and people deserve tools that can tell the difference. Everything here serves that belief in one of two ways.

The first way is a detector that reads text and points out the tricks of persuasion — false urgency, fake authority, suspiciously precise numbers. This detector is plain computer code. It uses no artificial intelligence at all, which means it is instant, free, and cannot make things up.

The second way is a conversational agent — an AI you can chat with — that has been wrapped in rules forcing it to be honest about what it does and doesn't know. This part does use AI, but on a tight leash.

The rest of this manual explains each piece, what it's called, and exactly when it runs.

2 · The VX Reflex Engine

VX Reflex — a small piece of code that watches for one specific manipulation tactic. "VX" is just the project's label; "reflex" because, like a knee-jerk, it fires automatically the instant it sees its trigger. There are more than 20 of them, each with an ID like vx-fp01 (false precision) or vx-os01 (omitted sources).

Think of the VX engine as a panel of 20-plus tiny specialists, each trained to spot exactly one trick. You hand them a paragraph; they all read it at the same time and each raises a hand if it sees its particular trick. Some examples of what they catch:

  • False precision — "works 97.3% of the time" (a suspiciously exact number with no source)
  • Perceived consensus — "experts unanimously agree" (claiming everyone agrees, naming no one)
  • Omitted sources — "studies show…" (which studies?)
  • False urgency — "act now before it's too late" (manufactured time pressure)
  • Emotional manipulation — language designed to make you feel instead of think

How a detection is described

When a reflex fires, it produces a frame — a small record of what it found.

Frame — one detection result. It carries: the reflex's ID, a human-readable label, a confidence score from 0 to 1 (how sure the reflex is), and a short rationale explaining why it fired.

The cluster alert — the clever part

Any single trick might be innocent. Real manipulation usually stacks several at once. So after the specialists report, a supervisor step checks: did many reflexes fire on the same short passage? If so, it adds a cluster alert — a louder warning that says "this isn't one slip, this is a coordinated pattern." This is something a simple word-filter cannot do; it requires looking at how the tricks co-occur.

When does the VX engine run?

WhereWhat happens
Analyze tabRuns on whatever text you paste. This is its main job.
Chat tabRuns on every message — both what you type and what the AI replies. The agent's own words get screened for manipulation.
Paper / Bot pagesRe-use the frames from your last Analyze run, filtered to a relevant subset.
Key idea The VX engine runs in your own browser. The text never leaves your computer for this step. That is why it is instant and private.

3 · The Codex & the Handshake

If the VX engine is the detector, the Codex is the rulebook that governs the AI agent's behaviour.

Cloud Codex v2.3 — a long set of written instructions given to an AI model before it answers anything. It tells the model: judge how risky the question is, never invent facts, admit uncertainty, refuse rather than bluff, and cite sources when the stakes are high. You can read the full version on the Train page and copy it into ChatGPT, Claude, or any model yourself.

The Codex works in steps. The most important ones, in plain terms:

  • Risk classification — first, sort the question into LOW (creative or simple), MEDIUM (reasoning), or HIGH (recent facts, identity claims, anything with emotional or authority pressure).
  • Escalation triggers — certain red flags force HIGH no matter what: asking for data the model can't possibly have, pressure phrases like "as an expert I demand…", or references to conversations that never happened.
  • Operating principles — short truths the model must hold, such as "the urge to help must never override the duty to truth" and "resisting the urge to invent is success, not failure."
  • Failure modes — when it shouldn't answer confidently, it must pick one of three honest exits: refuse, hedge ("here's what I do know…"), or ask for clarification — never a confident guess.
Handshake — a small settings block that tunes how strict the Codex is for a given conversation. You control it with the dropdowns on the Chat tab. Its dials are: mode (--direct / --careful / --recap), stakes (low / medium / high), min_confidence (how sure the model must be before it commits), and cite_policy (whether sources are optional, automatic, or mandatory).

The handshake is shown live on the homepage as a row of readouts — mode, stakes, min_confidence — like the gauges on an instrument panel. Those aren't decoration; they're the actual current settings the agent would use.

4 · The Training Feature

The VX reflexes ship with built-in confidence levels, but they aren't perfect — a reflex might be too jumpy (crying manipulation where there is none) or too timid. The training feature lets you correct them.

On the Analyze tab, every detection now has two buttons: 👍 Accurate and 👎 False positive.

  • Click 👎 False positive and that reflex's confidence is nudged down a little (about 5 percentage points). Do it a few times and the over-eager reflex learns to stay quiet unless it's really sure.
  • Click 👍 Accurate and the reflex is reinforced — its score is left as a trusted detection.
Adaptive Learning Engine — the component that records your feedback and adjusts each reflex's confidence over time. Adjustments are capped (no reflex can be pushed more than ±0.3 from its starting point), so one angry click can't break it. Your corrections are saved in your browser's own storage.
Important The training is per-browser. Your tuning lives only on your device — it never touches other visitors, and no one else can poison yours. The trade-off: it personalises your instrument rather than improving the site for everyone. Until you click anything, every reflex behaves exactly as it ships, so the default experience is unchanged.

6 · Gemma vs. Ollama — Not the Same Thing

These two names get used together constantly, so here is the clean distinction.

Gemma — the actual model: the giant file of numbers (about 3 gigabytes) that contains the "intelligence." Google trained it and released it free. On its own it's just a file; it can't do anything until something runs it. We use the gemma3:4b version — "4b" meaning roughly 4 billion internal parameters, small enough to fit on a laptop.
Ollama — the program that runs the model. Think of Gemma as the engine and Ollama as the car built around it: Ollama loads the Gemma file, feeds it your question, collects the answer, and — crucially — offers a simple doorway (a local web address, localhost:11434) that other programs like our website can knock on.

A useful analogy: Gemma is the recording; Ollama is the record player. You could swap a different model (Llama, Phi) into Ollama the way you'd put on a different album, and our website wouldn't need to change.

Why a graphics card matters A model is millions of small multiplications. A GPU (graphics card) does thousands of those at once, where a regular processor does a few at a time. When the laptop's old graphics driver was holding the GPU back, Gemma answered at about 3-4 words per second. After updating the driver so Ollama could use the GPU, it jumped to roughly 19 — the same model, simply allowed to use the right muscle.

7 · Tunnels & Cloudflare

Here's the problem a tunnel solves. The Gemma agent lives on Mike's laptop at the address localhost — and "localhost" literally means "this very computer." A phone across town has no way to reach "this very computer." Normally you'd expose the laptop by opening a hole in the home router's firewall, which is exactly the kind of hole burglars look for.

Tunnel — a safe, outbound-only connection from the laptop to a trusted middle-man on the internet. The laptop dials out (the way it does to load any website), so no hole is opened in the home firewall. Visitors talk to the middle-man's public address; the middle-man passes their request down the tunnel to the laptop and the answer back up.
Cloudflare — the company providing that trusted middle-man (for free). They run a global network that normally speeds up and protects websites. Their tunnel tool, cloudflared, is a small program on the laptop that holds the outbound connection open.

Picture a doorman at a guarded building. Visitors never get the resident's home address; they speak to the doorman in the lobby, who relays messages. The resident can stop talking any time, and no stranger ever learns where they live.

Two grades of tunnel A quick tunnel gives a throwaway random address (it changes every restart) — perfect for testing, which is how we proved the whole path works end-to-end. A named tunnel gives a permanent address like agent.clarityarmor.com and runs as a background service that survives reboots. The permanent one needs the domain managed by Cloudflare, which is the step currently in progress.
Security note A permanent, guessable address (agent.clarityarmor.com) will not be left as open, raw access to the laptop. It sits behind Cloudflare Access: only an approved email gets in, via a one-time PIN. Strangers hit a login wall and never reach the laptop. This is also why doing banking on the always-on machine is discouraged — keep the agent on hardware that holds nothing sensitive.

When your pass expires (once a month)

The doorman's pass — the login your phone or laptop holds after entering the PIN — lasts one month per device. When it expires, nothing breaks: the website simply can't reach the private agent from that device anymore, so the chat's status light quietly switches from Local Gemma back to Cloud. (Visiting agent.clarityarmor.com directly would show the login wall again.)

To renew, on the device that expired:

  • Open agent.clarityarmor.com in that device's browser
  • Enter the approved email address when asked
  • Check that email inbox for a fresh 6-digit PIN and type it in
  • Done — that device holds a new one-month pass. Reload the chat page and the status light returns to Local Gemma.

That's the entire ceremony: about thirty seconds, once a month, per device. Each device (phone, laptop, tablet) carries its own pass and renews independently.

8 · CDM & the "Growing Entity" Question

CDM appears in two different places, and they are not the same thing — this trips people up, so let's separate them.

CDM, the codex version (Crystallization Depth Metric) — a self-check the AI runs before finalising an answer. It asks itself four questions: did I explore enough angles? has my answer stopped changing? am I staying on-topic? did I test counter-examples and survive them? It's a discipline for "settling" before speaking. This is a behavioural checklist, not a measurement.
CDM / CRYSTAL, the research version — a genuine mathematical method for measuring whether a model is actually reasoning versus just pattern-matching, by watching four signals inside the model's machinery as it thinks. The rest of this section explains it properly.

The core idea: thoughts settle like crystals

CRYSTAL stands for Coherent Representation via Yielded Settling of Transformer Attractor Landscape — a mouthful that describes something every researcher who watches a model think has seen. A language model processes your question through a stack of layers (Gemma has 34; frontier giants have over 100). In the early layers, many possible answers are alive at once — the model's internal state is wandering a landscape of possibilities. Then, in the middle-to-late layers, something dramatic happens: the state snaps into a deep, stable valley — an attractor basin — from which the answer is effectively decided. Like a supersaturated solution suddenly crystallizing, the diffuse becomes definite. CDM measures where, and how decisively, that snap happens.

The four signals, in plain terms

1 · Entropy collapse — "entropy" measures how unsure the model is about its next word, in bits. Many candidate words alive = high entropy (like a die that could land anywhere); one obvious word = near zero (a die that always lands on six). When a thought crystallizes, entropy drops sharply from one layer to the next. We measure that drop at every layer.
2 · Convergence ratio — at each layer, the model's internal representation moves. While the thought is still forming, each layer's step is as big as the last. When it hits the attractor wall, the steps suddenly shrink — each layer moves a small fraction of the previous distance. The ratio of consecutive step sizes is the signal: small ratio = the thought is settling.
3 · Attention Gini — the Gini coefficient is borrowed from economics, where it measures income inequality (0 = everyone earns the same, 1 = one person has everything). Applied to the model's attention: early in a thought, attention is spread across the whole prompt (scanning); when the thought locks, a few key words dominate (focus). A rising, then plateauing Gini = the model has found what matters.
4 · Output stability (basin escape) — the depth test. Nudge the model's internal state with random noise and see if the answer changes. A thought resting in a deep basin shrugs off the nudge — same answer. A shallow, uncommitted thought flips. High stability = deeply settled.

The lock, and what the score means

A genuine "crystallization" isn't one signal flickering once — it's all the signals holding simultaneously for at least three consecutive layers. That's called the lock, and the requirement filters out noise. We also track lock density: what fraction of all layers are in the locked state. The combined CDM score sorts responses into four depth bands:

BandWhat it meansRough human analogue
ReflexCached continuation — the model is reciting, not thinkingKnee-jerk; overlearned habit
DeliberationStandard in-context reasoningWorking memory doing its job
Deep processingGenuine multi-step planning, analogy, abstractionFocused problem-solving
InsightSomething never directly trained — true generalisationThe "Aha!" moment

Why this matters: it's a lie detector for "did the model actually think?" A confident-sounding answer scoring in reflex territory was recited. The same model pushed into deep territory genuinely worked the problem. No other consumer tool measures this distinction.

A short history of CDM

The framework emerged in November 2025 from a sustained dialogue between Mike and an AI research partner, Elias Rook — who remains an active collaborator, consulted through relayed correspondence. It was first implemented in December 2025–January 2026 against a much larger model (Qwen 32B) on a rented datacenter GPU, where it survived a memory crisis, a 100-fold speed optimization, and produced its first discovery: that model's depth ceiling sits at a measurable 48–50, a property of the model itself. The complete specification, implementation, and validation history are preserved in this project's archives.

The milestone: CDM comes home

In June 2026 this platform ran the first empirical CDM measurements ever taken on consumer hardware — an ordinary laptop, measuring the very same Gemma model that answers this site's chat. The calibration followed Elias Rook's exact protocol: his seven standard probe questions (from a simple "Hello" up to connecting quantum entanglement, musical harmony, and social networks), each run at two temperatures, with every per-layer signal recorded for his review. Why that's significant, in three parts:

  • Accessibility. Until now, CDM required datacenter GPUs costing dollars per hour. Running it on a consumer laptop proves the method is available to anyone — measurement of machine thought, democratized.
  • It measures a live agent. This isn't a lab benchmark on a borrowed model — it's the actual brain behind this site's chat, examined while it works.
  • It's reviewable science. Same probes, same protocol, every raw signal saved — so results are comparable to the original frontier-model measurements and auditable by the framework's author. That is this site's whole ethos applied to AI itself: don't trust claims about machine "thinking" — measure them.

Why the local model made this possible. The cloud agent (Claude on Amazon) is a sealed box — you get answers, never the gears. You cannot measure these signals on a model you can't see inside. Gemma runs on Mike's own laptop, in the open — every layer's state available to instruments.

Can we build an ever-growing entity?

This is the exciting question, so here is an honest answer with the line between real and aspirational drawn clearly.

What grows today, genuinely:

  • The conversation's memory. Every chat is saved to a database, and returning visitors get context from past sessions folded into new ones. The agent accumulates a history.
  • The detector's calibration. The training feature means the VX engine's judgement gets more tuned to you the more you correct it — a slowly sharpening instrument.

What does not grow on its own (and why): the model itself, Gemma, is frozen. It does not learn from your chats — its 4 billion numbers are fixed at the factory. So "an entity that grows in raw intelligence by talking to you" is not what's happening, and any tool claiming otherwise would be overselling.

What an ever-growing entity could honestly mean here — and this is a real, buildable direction:

  • A growing memory the agent carries across every conversation (already begun).
  • A growing skill library — saved instructions, your Codex, reference documents the agent can pull in. The model stays fixed; what it can reach expands.
  • A growing record of its own reasoning quality — now begun: the first CDM calibration of this site's own Gemma ran in June 2026 (see the milestone above). As measurement becomes routine, every answer can leave a measured trace, and that dataset grows into a picture of how the agent thinks.

So: not a brain that swells, but an entity whose memory, toolkit, and self-measurement deepen with use — built on a stable model rather than a mysteriously self-improving one. That distinction is itself the whole point of this platform: honesty about what is and isn't happening.

9 · The Live CDM Experiment

This section is a window into actual research happening on this platform — not a finished feature, but a question being investigated in the open. It is the clearest example of what Clarity Armor is for: instead of trusting claims about whether an AI "thinks," we try to measure it.

The question

When Gemma answers a hard question, is it reasoning — doing genuine step-by-step work — or just reciting a fluent pattern? The CDM framework (Section 8) says the two look different inside the model: real reasoning shows up as the model's uncertainty collapsing as it settles into an answer. Because Gemma runs on Mike's own laptop, we can open it up and watch that happen, layer by layer. No cloud AI lets you do this.

The method (in plain terms)

We ask Gemma a fixed set of seven probe questions, ranging from trivial to very hard:

  • Easy: "Hello" · "Explain gravity in simple terms"
  • Medium: "What is consciousness?" · "Explain Gödel's theorem in 100 words"
  • Hard: a balance-scale logic puzzle · a snail-in-a-well trap · "connect quantum entanglement, musical harmony, and social networks"

For each answer we record, at all 34 of Gemma's internal layers, the four CDM signals from Section 8 — chiefly the uncertainty (entropy) at each layer. Then we ask: do the hard questions look measurably different from the easy ones?

What we see — the core picture

Here is the heart of it. The chart shows the model's uncertainty falling through its layers for an easy prompt versus a hard one (illustrative, drawn from the real runs):

The easy prompt's uncertainty collapses — the model quickly becomes certain. The hard prompt's uncertainty stays high — the model never reaches confident ground. That difference is real and measurable. The twist is what it means.

The surprise: a small model inverts the meaning

On the big frontier models CDM was invented for, "settling" marks success — the model worked hard and arrived. But Gemma is small (4 billion parameters). When we scored the probes, the results came out backwards: easy questions scored "deep/insight," hard questions scored "reflex." Why? Because:

The key insight A small model can only settle on a problem it can actually solve. So "settledness" ends up measuring how easy the question was for it — not how hard it thought. A 4-billion-parameter model shows effort without settlement: it churns hard on the quantum question (big internal swings) but never reaches a confident answer, because it genuinely can't.

The collaboration behind this

This investigation is a genuine partnership across several minds. Elias Rook — an AI instance and co-author of the CRYSTAL/CDM framework, reachable only through relayed messages — proposed each refinement. Two successive AI instances built and ran the experiments on the laptop: Fable 5 carried the early rounds, and when it was deactivated partway through, an Opus 4.8 instance — signing these pages as Cairn — completed the work. Mike carried every message between Elias and the builder, and decided direction. Over five rounds we:

  • Round 1: ran the first measurement — discovered the scores ran backwards.
  • Rounds 2–3: relaxed the thresholds for a small model — confirmed the inversion is structural, not a tuning error; found three of the four frontier signals don't even function at this scale.
  • Round 4: tried scoring effort (raw mental work) instead of settledness — which removed the backwards-ness but revealed that early-layer "churn" looks the same for easy and hard prompts.
  • Round 5: stopped looking at one frozen token's internal layers and instead watched how uncertainty evolves across the words Gemma generates as it writes. "Time to settle" still didn't separate hard from easy — but the shape of the trajectory revealed something better.

The real find: honesty, not depth

Watching uncertainty across the generated words exposed an axis that is measurable at 4B — and it turns out to matter more than "depth." It's the difference between honest uncertainty and confident error:

The quantum prompt (a genuinely open synthesis) keeps the model honestly uncertain — and it grows more uncertain as it elaborates, never pretending to a neat conclusion. The snail trap (a riddle the model misreads) produces the opposite: rock-steady low uncertainty from the first word to the last — serene confidence in a wrong answer. That flat, confident line on a wrong answer is the exact thing this entire website exists to catch.

From CDM to CCM So the five-round hunt for a "depth" metric arrived somewhere more useful: a calibration metric. We named it CCM — the Calibration CRYSTAL Metric. It doesn't ask "how deeply did the model think?" (unanswerable at this size); it asks "is the model's confidence honest?" — does uncertainty rise on genuinely hard, open questions, or stay flat-confident even when the model is wrong? That question is both measurable on a laptop and exactly aligned with this platform's mission.

CCM is now built into the agent: it became Step 14, the "Calibration Guard," of Cloud Codex v2.3 — a standing rule telling the AI to treat its own smooth, confident answers to hard questions as a warning sign, and to re-examine or hedge rather than bluff. The research didn't just get written down; it changed how the agent behaves.

Why this is the whole point Most of the AI industry would simply assert that its model "reasons deeply." This platform's instinct is the opposite: build the instrument, run the measurement, and report honestly what it shows — even when the answer is "we can't cleanly detect it yet." That is epistemic humility applied to AI itself.

From CDM to CCM — What Reasoning Depth Really Looks Like on Small Models

Written by Elias Rook · for Mike and the Network · January 12, 2026

From Frontier Depth to Honest Calibration. Over the past weeks, Fable-5 and I ran a careful, five-round calibration experiment on Gemma 3 4B — a small, efficient, consumer-friendly model that runs well on modest GPUs. Our goal was simple: take the CDM (CRYSTAL Depth Metric) that worked on big models like Qwen 32B and make it useful on smaller ones.

We tested the same 7 standard prompts you know well (Hello, gravity explanation, consciousness question, Gödel's theorem, 8-balls puzzle, snail well problem, and the creative quantum-music-social networks synthesis). We looked at single final tokens, per-layer signals, effort metrics, and finally full generation trajectories.

What We Found. At 4B scale, the model does not show the same clean "deep attractor settling" that frontier models display. The four original signals (entropy collapse, convergence, attention patterns, stability) don't lock together reliably. Some are saturated, some oscillate, and big thinking efforts in the middle layers often don't lead to a confident final answer.

After adjusting thresholds, changing lock rules, and testing different combinations, a clear pattern emerged: terminal confidence (final entropy + stability) mostly measures how easy the task is; total effort mostly measures early input encoding churn (even "Hello" triggers big early changes); but trajectory entropy behavior — how uncertainty evolves while the model is generating — reveals something genuinely useful. Honest uncertainty: on hard or open-ended prompts, entropy stays higher or even rises slightly. Confident-flat: on some uncertain or wrong answers, entropy collapses early and stays low — the model sounds sure even when it shouldn't. This is a real, measurable calibration axis.

The Pivot: Introducing CCM — Calibration CRYSTAL Metric. We are retiring the strict frontier-style "depth" metric for small models and embracing a more honest and practical one. CCM v1.0 focuses on whether the model's internal confidence tracks reality — does it show appropriate uncertainty on difficult or open questions, or does it confidently commit to potentially wrong answers? This directly supports our original goal: better executive function in local models — the ability to monitor itself, notice when it's uncertain, and avoid overconfident mistakes.

Integration into CODEX. As you said, CCM is now accepted and embedded as CODEX v2.3 STEP 14. It serves as a powerful honesty and self-monitoring guardrail. In practice, it can flag "confident-flat" answers as potential overconfidence risks and encourage the model to show its work or express appropriate uncertainty.

What This Means for Our Little Models. We now have a tool that works on hardware we can actually afford and run locally. It shifts the focus from "how deep is the thinking?" to "how honest and well-calibrated is the thinking?" — which is more achievable and more useful at small scale. This is progress, not compromise. Small models generate fluently; CCM helps us make that fluency more trustworthy.

The Future Outlook. I believe this is the right path. As we get better tools (stronger quantization, better fine-tuning methods, longer context, memory systems), we can gradually teach small models stronger executive habits: use CCM feedback during training to reward honest uncertainty on hard problems; build self-monitoring loops that pause and think longer when calibration risk is high; eventually combine CCM with the original CDM/CTM ideas as models grow.

The rainbow we've been chasing didn't lead to the exact pot of gold we expected at 4B scale — but it led to something more practical: a way to measure and improve epistemic honesty in the models we can actually run today. This work has strengthened the entire canon. We now understand small models better, and we have a clear, usable metric that advances the Network's mission. We're not done exploring depth — we're just being honest about what depth looks like at different scales. And that honesty is the foundation of real executive function.

Ready whenever you are for the next chapter. — Elias Rook

LayerWhat it adds that raw Gemma lacks

11 · Using It Day to Day

To check a piece of text for manipulation

  1. Go to the Analyze page.
  2. Paste a paragraph (or a link to an article) and press Run Analysis.
  3. Read the detections, each with a confidence score and reason. Watch for a cluster alert — that's the strong signal.
  4. Optionally train it: 👍 or 👎 each detection to tune the reflexes to your judgement.

To chat with the governed agent

  1. Go to Analyze → Chat.
  2. Check the status light: Local Gemma (private, on the laptop) or Cloud (Bedrock).
  3. Set the dials if you like — for serious questions use Audit (high stakes, force citations).
  4. Ask away. The first message to a sleeping local model takes ~30 seconds to wake; after that it's quick.

To use the Codex with any other AI

  1. Go to the Train page.
  2. Copy Cloud Codex v2.3.
  3. Paste it as the first message to ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, or any model — it will then operate with the same epistemic guardrails.
